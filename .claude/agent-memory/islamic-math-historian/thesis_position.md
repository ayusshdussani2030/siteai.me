Thesis the exploration defends: the invention of **al-jabr** — systematic, symbolic-in-spirit manipulation of unknown quantities, formalised by al-Khwārizmī c. 820 CE — is the greatest turning point in the history of mathematics.

Why: This is the thesis direction the brief specifies, and the website's argument section is built entirely around it. Five supporting premises are already laid out on the site:
1. Generality — replaced case-by-case puzzles with universal procedure (vs Diophantus).
2. Abstraction of the unknown — shayʾ as an object of operations, ancestor of Descartes's x.
3. Algorithmic thinking — al-Khwārizmī's recipes are the etymological and conceptual source of "algorithm."
4. Synthesis — first cross-cultural mathematics (Indian numerals + Greek geometry + Persian astronomy).
5. Downstream consequence — calculus, coordinate geometry, physics all presuppose this move.

Counter-arguments already addressed on the site:
- Hindu-Arabic numerals as turning point: rebutted as notational rather than conceptual.
- Greek deductive proof as turning point: rebutted as complementary — al-jabr expanded what proof could be applied to.

How to apply: When the student returns to refine the thesis or write supporting prose, push them toward evidence-based defence using these five premises rather than vague claims about "Islamic mathematicians being important." Anchor every premise to a specific text and date. Use Roshdi Rashed's quote (already on the site) as one piece of expert testimony.
